Assessment vs Evaluation — What is the Difference?

New words: Assessment = checking how you are learning. Evaluation = giving a final mark or grade. Feedback = comments to help you improve.

Assessment

Helps you learn and improve.

Your teacher looks at your work and gives you feedback so you can do better next time.

Example: Your teacher says, "Your bridge sketch needs labels. Add the names of each part."

Evaluation

Decides how good your final work is.

Your teacher gives a grade or mark based on set rules (called criteria).

Example: Your teacher marks your bridge project and gives you a B grade.

Key idea: Assessment helps you improve BEFORE the final grade. This writing coach is an assessment tool — it helps you learn how to write better answers in Technology class.

Key Verbs — The Most Important Word in a Question

A verb is an action word. In assessment questions, the verb tells you WHAT to do and HOW MUCH to write.

What is a verb? A verb is a "doing" word. For example: list, describe, explain, evaluate. In a question, the verb tells you what your teacher wants you to do.
List / Name
Just write the names.
Short answer.
Least detail
Describe
Say what something is like.
Give some details.
Some detail
Explain
Say WHY or HOW.
Give reasons.
More detail
Evaluate
Judge if something is good or bad.
Give evidence and reasons.
Most detail
Easy way to remember: Higher-level verbs (like explain and evaluate) need longer, more detailed answers. Lower-level verbs (like list) need shorter answers.
📖 LEARN

A.L.A.R.M. — A Learning and Responding Matrix

ALARM helps you understand HOW MUCH detail a question needs. Each level builds on the one before it:

What does "matrix" mean? A matrix is a table or chart that helps you organise information. ALARM is a chart that matches action words (verbs) to how much detail you need to write.
A — Acquire
identify, list, name, recall, label

Just remember and name things

L — Link
describe, state, define, outline

Connect ideas together, give details

A — Analyse
explain, compare, contrast, discuss

Break apart, find reasons and patterns

R — Respond
evaluate, justify, assess, recommend

Make judgements with evidence

M — Make
create, design, develop, construct

Build or produce something new

V.S.C.C. — Breaking Down Any Question

Every question in Technology has 4 parts. Finding them helps you know exactly what to write.

Why do we break down questions? Sometimes questions are long and confusing. When you break them into 4 parts, they become easier to understand and answer.

V — Verb

The action word. What do I need to DO?

S — Subject

The main topic. What is this about?

C — Content

The specific details. What information should I include?

C — Concept

The big idea. What is the deeper meaning?

Worked Example

Question: "Describe the materials you chose for your bridge and explain why they are suitable."

V — Describe & Explain

Give details and say WHY

S — Materials

The things you used to build

C — Your bridge

The specific bridge you designed

C — Suitability

Why these materials are right for the job

ALARM Level: Link + Analyse (because the verbs are "describe" and "explain")

✏️ WRITING TASK

Paragraph Builder: Describe Your Bridge Design

Question: "Describe the bridge you designed and explain why you chose your materials."
Sentence starters to help you:
Emerging: "My bridge is a ___ bridge. I used ___ to build it."
Developing: "I designed a ___ bridge because ___. The main materials I used were ___."
Proficient: "For my bridge project, I chose to design a ___ bridge. I selected ___ as my primary material because ___."
1
Topic Sentence
Name your bridge type and say what it looks like. E.g. "I designed a truss bridge using paddle pop sticks and PVA glue."
2
Material Choice
Say what materials you used and WHY. E.g. "I chose paddle pop sticks because they are strong, light and easy to cut."
3
Design Features
Describe the key features. E.g. "My bridge uses triangles in the trusses to spread the load evenly."
4
Conclusion
Sum up your design. E.g. "Overall, I believe my bridge design is effective because it uses strong shapes and suitable materials."
📖 EXAMPLES

What Does Each Grade Level Look Like?

Read these example responses. Notice how the higher grades have more detail and reasons.

A — Outstanding
"I designed a truss bridge using paddle pop sticks, PVA glue and string. I chose paddle pop sticks as my primary material because they are lightweight yet strong enough to withstand compression forces when arranged in triangular shapes. The triangular trusses distribute the load evenly across the bridge, which increases stability. I also used string for additional tension support along the bottom chord. Overall, my design is effective because it combines strong geometric shapes with suitable materials, although I would add cross-bracing in a future version to further improve stability."
Why A? Detailed explanation, uses key vocabulary (compression, tension, stability), evaluates strengths AND suggests improvements.
C — Sound
"I designed a truss bridge. I used paddle pop sticks and glue. I chose sticks because they are strong. My bridge has triangles to make it stronger. I think my bridge is okay because it holds some weight."
Why C? Names the bridge type and materials, gives a basic reason, but lacks detail and technical vocabulary.
E — Limited
"I made a bridge. It is made of sticks."
Why E? Very short, no detail, no reasons, no key vocabulary.

Feedback Sandwich: Reflect on Your Learning

What is a Feedback Sandwich? It is 3 parts: (1) What went well, (2) What could be better, (3) What you will do next. Like bread-filling-bread!
W
WWW — What Went Well
"Something I did well was..." or "I am proud that I..."
E
EBI — Even Better If
"I could improve by..." or "Next time I will try to..."
N
YNS — Your Next Steps
"My goal for the bridge project is..." or "I will practise..."
